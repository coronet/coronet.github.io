I enjoy understanding complex systems well enough to describe them to other people in simple terms. I'm passionate about friendly APIs, highly-available systems, and service-oriented engineering platforms.

Education

In 2007, I got a B.S. in C.S. from the University of Wisconsin - Madison.

Work

After graduating, I joined the Windows Networking team at Microsoft. I contributed to the System.Net modules of the .NET Framework, and built the low-level networking APIs for Microsoft Silverlight (RIP). I learned a lot about asynchronous programming models, the HTTP protocol, and windbg. I engaged with customers building low-latency and large-scale networked systems to help them optimize performance and resource utilization.

 

In 2010 I left to join the E-Commerce Platform Services team at Amazon.com. I built a highly-available revision-controlled database for business metadata, growing it from a single-use-case prototype build on Dynamo to a company-wide hosted service handling hundreds of thousands of requests per second across multiple regions. I led the development of a distributed log-based storage service, the rearchitecture of the database to use it instead of Dynamo, and the zero-downtime migration to the new storage backend. I learned a lot about the Java garbage collector, the CAP theorem, and self-healing systems that won't page people at 2am.

 

In 2013 I moved over to Amazon Web Services, where I contributed to AWS's open-source developer tools, helped service teams design developer-friendly APIs, engaged with customers building performant and scalable applications on the AWS platform, and once got paid to talk about my cat in public. I learned a lot about open source licenses, semantic versioning, and backwards compatibility in an Internet-scale distributed system.

 

In 2015 I left to join Salesforce. I helped to found a software-engineering function within the security organization, architected a highly-available HSM-backed key management service, and partnered with teams across the company to roll out encryption at rest and in transit for sensitive customer data. I learned a lot about random number generation, FIPS 104-2, and how to win friends and influence people.

In 2018 I transitioned to a new role at Salesforce, working with the office of the CTO to clarify and simplify the company's architecture. I do this through writing and speaking about cross-cutting strategic initiatives, consulting with teams in architectural transition, and organizing working groups of other architects to drive alignment across the company.

I've worked remotely since 2016, and VERY remotely (from Australia) since 2018. By all accounts I've gotten reasonably good at it.

Other Stuff

I regularly speak at external meet-ups and conferences on technical topics. From 2015-2018 I organized the Seattle chapter of Papers We Love, a community built around reading, discussing, and learning more about academic computer science papers.
