David likes finding the right way to talk about hard concepts using simple words. He's passionate about beautiful APIs, well-architected developer platforms, and highly-available distributed systems.

Education

In 2007, he got a B.S. in C.S. from the University of Wisconsin - Madison.

Work

After graduating David joined the Windows Networking team at Microsoft. He contributed to the System.Net modules of the .NET Framework, and built the low-level networking APIs for Microsoft Silverlight (RIP). He learned a lot about asynchronous programming models, the HTTP protocol, and windbg. He engaged with customers building low-latency and large-scale networked systems to help them optimize performance and resource utilization.

In 2010 he left to join the E-Commerce Platform Services team at Amazon.com. He built a highly-available revision-controlled database for business rules, growing it from a single-tenant prototype built on a successor of Dynamo to a company-wide hosted service built on top of a durable distributed log (which he led the development of). He learned way too much about the Java garbage collector, the CAP theorem, and self-healing systems that won't page you at 2am. By the time he left, the service was used by ~100 teams and serving hundreds of thousands of requests per second with sub-millisecond latency.

In 2013 he began working for Amazon Web Services, where he contributed to AWS's open-source developer tools, engaged with customers building performant and scalable applications on the AWS platform, helped service teams design developer-friendly APIs, and once got paid to talk about his cat in public.

In 2015 he moved across the street to work at Salesforce. He has delusions of grandeur, and hopes he'll be able to open source them one day soon.

Other Stuff

David organizes the Seattle chapter of Papers We Love, a community built around reading, discussing, and learning more about academic computer science papers.
